Bike Crashes Dataset

We’ll first implement descriptive statistics on different variables on the dataset, specifically X and Y axis, RequestType, RequestDate, and UserType.

X and Y represent the location of the traffic crash reported. We could either map the data or directly use the interactive map provided by Vision Zero Boston, and study the if there’s any consistency in location. Request Type is a nominal data. These are a set of reasons of the accident chosen by the reporter, such as “it’s too far / too many lanes to cross”, and “bike facilities don’t exist or need improvement”. Request Date is an interval data that describes when the incident occurs. Depending on the complexity, we can bread down the time into year, month, or even time of the day. We could study if there’s a change in traffic crash over the past few years since the data was recorded, or examine if there’s a peak in traffic crash during a specific period of the year. Alternatively, we could study if most accidents happen in the morning, or at night. UserType is used to filter out the type of crashes we want to study. With these data, inferential studies could be conducted to examine the correlations between variables.

One of the tools we use in this project is Vision Zero Boston that display traffic crashes on the map. This is from VisionZeroBoston.org created by City of Boston, in order to focus on the city resources to improve strategies to eliminate fatal and serious traffic crashes in the city by 2030. The website itself does not provide too much technical information, but it gives a broad introduction of what the program is for and what actions we as residents can do. From looking at the source code, the interactive mapping tool used on the site imported data from the dataset of City of Boston, and used Google Mapping Tool API to stack the selected data on top of the map. ArcGIS Online is then used to build and share interactive map.
